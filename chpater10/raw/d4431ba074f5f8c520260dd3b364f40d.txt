        </div><p>Hanoi (AFP) - Vietnam on Thursday called for the "immediate withdrawal" of a Chinese ship in the South China Sea, as the standoff over the disputed waters intensified.</p><p>Beijing last week issued a new call for Hanoi to respect its claims to the resource-rich region -- which have historically been contested by Vietnam as well as Taiwan, the Philippines, Malaysia and Brunei.</p><p>Hanoi responded by saying it had sent several messages to Beijing insisting that a Chinese survey ship vacate its waters, and doubled down Thursday with new demands for the vessel's removal.</p><p>"Vietnam has had several appropriate diplomatic exchanges...requesting immediate withdrawal from Vietnam's exclusive economic zone," a foreign ministry spokeswoman told reporters, while refusing to disclose the ship's precise location.</p><p>"Vietnam resolutely and persistently protects our sovereign rights...by peaceful means on the basis of international laws," Le Thi Thu Hang added. </p><p>The ship, owned by the government-run China Geological Survey, begun research around the contested Spratly Islands on July 3, according to the US-based Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS). </p><p>Before it was spotted, a Chinese coast guard vessel also patrolled near Vietnamese supply ships in a "threatening manner", CSIS said.</p><p>China has not confirmed the presence of its ships in the area.</p><p>Beijing invokes its so-called nine-dash line to justify its apparently historic rights to the waterway, and has previously built up artificial islands as well as installed airstrips and military equipment in the region.</p><p>The line runs as far as 2,000 kilometres (1,200 miles) from the Chinese mainland to within a few hundred kilometres of the Philippines, Malaysia and Vietnam.</p><p>In 2014 Beijing moved an oil rig into waters claimed by Hanoi, sparking deadly, anti-China protests across Vietnam. </p><p>The latest stand-off in the Sea prompted a swift rebuke from the United States over the weekend, calling for an end to China's "bullying behaviour". </p><p>"China's repeated provocative actions aimed at the offshore oil and gas development of other claimant states threaten regional energy security," the US State Department said Saturday. </p><p>The United States has long called for freedom of navigation in the South China Sea, and on Thursday said it sailed a warship through the Taiwan Strait. </p></div></div></main></article></div><div class="article-action-container horizontal"><div class="article-action"
