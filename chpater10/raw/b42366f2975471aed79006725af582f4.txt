Sudan July 27, 2019. REUTERS/Mohamed Nureldin Abdallah</p></div>Five Sudanese demonstrators including students were shot dead Monday during a rally attended by schoolchildren against a shortage of bread, a day before protest leaders and the ruling generals hold fresh talks.</p>
<p>The authorities announced a night-time curfew in four towns following the deaths in the central town of Al-Obeid.</p>
<p>The generals and protest leaders have already inked a power-sharing deal that aims to set up a joint civilian-military ruling body which in turn would install civilian rule.</p>
<div class="ad-align-none"><div class="box">
        		    <div style="width: 100%; overflow: hidden">
			    <div id='ventra-dfp-2' class='dfp-ad ad-container' style='margin: 15px auto; height:250px; width: 300px;'>

		    </div>
	    </div></div>
<p>That is the main demand of a nationwide protest movement that led to the April ouster of longtime leader Omar al-Bashir and has since demanded that the military council which took his place cede power to civilians.</p>
<p>But on the eve of Tuesday&#8217;s talks aimed at resolving the remaining issues over transferring power, five protesters including four students were killed in Al-Obeid, the capital of North Kordofan state, a doctors committee linked to the protest movement said.</p>
<p>&#8220;Five martyrs succumbed to direct wounds from sniper bullets during a peaceful rally in Al-Obeid,&#8221; the committee said in a statement.</p>
<p>A key protest group, the Sudanese Professionals Association, said that &#8220;live ammunition&#8221; had been used against what it said was a student rally.</p>
<p>&#8220;We are calling on all citizens and medics to go to the emergency ward of Al-Obeid hospital and other hospitals that are receiving the wounded from the live ammunition fired on the rally of school students,&#8221; the group said on its Facebook page.</p>
<p>The office of North Kordofan&#8217;s governor said a curfew would be imposed indefinitely from 9:00 pm to 6:00 am in four towns including Al-Obeid starting on Monday, adding that all schools in the province had been told to suspend classes.</p>
<p><strong> Call for suspending talks </strong><br />
Residents of Al-Obeid said the rally was against a shortage of bread and fuel in the town.</p>
<p>&#8220;For the past few days there has been a shortage of fuel and bread,&#8221; a resident told AFP by telephone.</p>
<p>&#8220;School children were affected as there is no transport to help them reach their schools. Today, they staged a rally and when it reached downtown there were shots fired.&#8221;</p>
<p>It was the tripling of bread prices that initially triggered protests against Bashir, which later turned into a nationwide movement.</p>
<p>The town of Al-Obeid had not witnessed any major rallies against Bashir even as provinces, cities and towns were swept by the campaign against his rule.</p>
<p>Monday&#8217;s deaths triggered calls to suspend the planned talks.</p>
<p>&#8220;We cannot sit at the negotiating table with those allowing the killing of revolutionaries,&#8221; Siddig Youssef, a prominent protest leader, said in a statement.</p>
<p>Protest leaders say Tuesday&#8217;s talks will cover issues including the powers of the joint civilian-military ruling body, the deployment of security forces and immunity for generals over protest-related violence.</p>
<p>The power-sharing deal already agreed on July 17 provides for the establishment of a new governing body of six civilians and five generals.</p>
<p>It will then oversee the formation of a transitional civilian government and parliament to govern for 39 months, after which elections will be held.</p>
<p><strong> Impartial probe </strong><br />
In Khartoum, demonstrators have been rallying again since Saturday, when an investigation found that security officers, including some from the feared Rapid Support Forces, carried out a deadly crackdown on a protest camp without any orders from their superiors.</p>
<p>Shortly before dawn on June 3, gunmen in military fatigues raided the site of the weeks-long sit-in outside the military headquarters in Khartoum, shooting and beating protesters.</p>
<p>Doctors linked to the protest movement say the raid left 127 people dead and scores wounded.</p>
<p>But the joint investigation by prosecutors and the ruling military council that took power following Bashir&#8217;s ouster found that just 17 people were killed on June 3, with a total of 87 dying between that day and June 10.</p>
<p>The probe identified eight officers involved in the violent crackdown on the protest camp, including three from the Rapid Support Forces.</p>
<p>The eight officers face charges of crimes against humanity, chief investigator Fatah al-Rahman Saeed told reporters on Saturday.</p>
<p>But protest leaders have rejected the findings, saying the inquiry exonerated the military council and gave a far lower death toll than their own figures.</p>
<p>Saeed&#8217;s investigation &#8220;was commissioned by the military council, this is challenging its integrity as the military council itself is accused in this case,&#8221; said the Sudanese Professionals Association.</p>
<p>Angry demonstrators have called for an independent investigation into the raid.</p>
<p>On Sunday, scores of protesters chanting the months-long protest movement&#8217;s catchcry of &#8220;Freedom, peace, justice!&#8221; rallied in Khartoum&#8217;s eastern Burri district, witnesses said, adding that riot police swiftly dispersed the crowd with tear gas.</p>
<p>The country&#8217;s ruling generals have insisted they did not order the dispersal of the sit-in.</p>
