Stevens-Rumann, 33, told CBC News. &quot;I got really interested in thinking about how we&#x27;re managing our landscapes, and how [the] decisions we&#x27;re making even on the fire line are influencing how those ecosystems are recovering after.&quot;</p>  <p>Her research has taken her from the charred forests of America&#x27;s Rocky Mountain ranges all the way to the Pacific Northwest, just south of the B.C. border.</p>  <div><span><ul class="similarLinks"><li class="similarListItem"><a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/british-columbia/lightning-wildfires-bc-july-2019-1.5221533" data-contentid="" class="similarLink"><span class="similarLinkText">1,000 lightning strikes spark 10 new wildfires in B.C.</span></a></li></ul></span></div>  <p>What she&#x27;s found: certain tree species are having a tough time growing back in areas that have been affected by wildfires due to warming temperatures — a discovery that could have major implications for both the forestry sector and long-term climate change targets.</p>  <div><span><figure class="imageMedia image full"><div class="placeholder"><div class="placeholderImage" style="padding-bottom:55.65%"></div><img alt="" src="https://i.cbc.ca/1.4891455.1541435998!/fileImage/httpImage/image.PNG_gen/derivatives/original_780/the-south-stikine-river-fire.PNG"/></div><figcaption class="image-caption">The South Stikine River fire pictured just east of Telegraph Creek, B.C. in August 2018. <!-- --> <!-- -->(B.C. Her research found that lower elevation trees had a tough time naturally regenerating in areas that burned between 2000 and 2015 compared with sites affected between 1985 and 1999, largely due to drier weather conditions.</p>  <p>More recently, <a href="https://esajournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/epdf/10.1002/ecs2.2568">a 2019 study</a> written by her colleague Kerry Kemp found that both Douglas fir and Ponderosa pine seedlings in the Idaho&#x27;s Rocky Mountains — just south of B.C. — were also struggling in low-lying burned areas due to warmer temperatures, leading to lower tree densities.</p>  <div><span><ul class="similarLinks"><li class="similarListItem"><a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/british-columbia/wildfire-forest-fire-bc-wildfire-service-cawston-b-c-1.5225333" data-contentid="" class="similarLink"><span class="similarLinkText">Wildfire south of Cawston, B.C., grows to 60 hectares</span></a></li></ul></span></div>  <p>Both studies attribute climate change to be the lead cause of why the trees are struggling to grow back in certain fire-scarred areas.</p>  <p>Stevens-Rumann says there are many similar forests facing the same challenges in B.C.&#x27;s Southern Interior, while repeat wildfires in the province are likely also to inhibit regrowth in many areas.</p>  <p>As a result, some ecosystems will no longer be able to support tree species. Instead they may convert to grasslands, she said.</p>  <div><span><ul class="similarLinks"><li class="similarListItem"><a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/british-columbia/scorching-heat-in-the-forecast-as-crews-battle-small-wildfire-near-osoyoos-1.5226016" data-contentid="" class="similarLink"><span class="similarLinkText">Scorching heat in the forecast as crews battle small wildfire near Osoyoos</span></a></li></ul></span></div>  <p>&quot;You have an old fire scar, and maybe there&#x27;s a really great amount of tree regeneration ... but then in one of the fires you&#x27;ve had in the last couple of years, it&#x27;s re-burned all of those trees that are only 10-20 years old, and now you&#x27;re resetting whether or not it&#x27;s going to have tree regeneration again,&quot; she said.</p>  <div><span><figure class="imageMedia image full"><div class="placeholder"><div class="placeholderImage" style="padding-bottom:64.35%"></div><img alt="" src="https://i.cbc.ca/1.4871684.1540064878!/fileImage/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/original_780/wildfires-bc-20180815-checkup.jpg"/></div><figcaption class="image-caption">Verne Tom photographs a wildfire burning along a logging road approximately 20 kilometres southwest of Fort St. James, B.C., on Aug. 15.<!-- --> <!-- -->(Darryl Dyck/Canadian Press)</figcaption></figure></span></div>  <h2>Losing forests</h2>  <p>The concerns were echoed by University of British Columbia forestry professor and associate dean Sally Aitken, who co-authored a study that mapped out how landscapes in B.C. are changing in the face of changing climate.</p>  <p>Aitken said many areas in the province that were burned during the record-breaking 2017-2018 wildfire seasons were also recovering from previous wildfires.</p>  <p>When juvenile or seedlings burn before they&#x27;re mature enough to drop seeds, forest may experience what&#x27;s known as seed source shortfall.</p>  <div><span><ul class="similarLinks"><li class="similarListItem"><a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/british-columbia/city-of-vancouver-air-quality-plan-1.5225909" data-contentid="" class="similarLink"><span class="similarLinkText">Vancouver to offer rooms with filtered air when air quality drops</span></a></li></ul></span></div>  <p>&quot;When those trees are too young to really produce much in terms of seeds, if it burns again, then you&#x27;re really in a seed source shortfall,&quot; Aitken told CBC News.</p>  <p>Right now, upwards of 300 million trees are being planted in B.C. annually, a significant portion of it designated for areas that have previously burned. But Aitken says the fertility of landscape is inconsistent, as dry heat in some areas has stripped the soil of its ability to support trees.</p>  <p>&quot;Some areas will likely convert from being forests to being more like grasslands at those very dry margins at the extent of the forest,&quot; she said.</p>  <div><span><figure class="imageMedia image full"><div class="placeholder"><div class="placeholderImage" style="padding-bottom:66.45%"></div><img alt="" src="https://i.cbc.ca/1.4806139.1536330650!/cpImage/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/original_780/wildfires-bc-20180817.jpg"/></div><figcaption class="image-caption">Smoke rises from an area burned by the Shovel Lake wildfire near Endako, B.C., late Thursday, August 16, 2018. At more than Shovel Lake wildfire 922 square kilometres in size it is one of the largest of the more than 500 fires still burning across the province. <!-- --> <!-- -->(Darryl Dyck/Canadian Press)</figcaption></figure></span></div>  <h2>Implications</h2>  <p>While some areas at higher elevations are experiencing regrowth, both Aitken and Stevens-Rumann say some ecosystems will no longer be able to support tree species that have historically stood tall over the landscape.</p>  <p>With more grasslands dispersed through the province, the forests&#x27; ability to sequester carbon from the atmosphere is hampered, they said.</p>  <div><span></span></div>  <p>&quot;We can get unhealthier forests, and [they are] not going to grow as well, and going to provide potentially a positive feedback loop for climate change,&quot; said Aitken.</p>  <div><span><figure class="imageMedia image full"><div class="placeholder"><div class="placeholderImage" style="padding-bottom:66.61%"></div><img alt="" src="https://i.cbc.ca/1.4788949.1534514800!/cpImage/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/original_780/wildfires-bc-20180816.jpg"/></div><figcaption class="image-caption">Trees burned by the Shovel Lake wildfire are seen near Endako, B.C. on Aug. 16.
