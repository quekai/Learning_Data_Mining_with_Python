class="poster"><img src="https://cdn.newsapi.com.au/image/v1/f22e04d455348b88f48291fe984a8970" alt="The woman with 2500 personalities (60 Minutes)"></div><div class="description"><p>Jeni Haynes src="https://cdn.newsapi.com.au/image/v1/aee3bc033b3919199e0dbcfa5e128a50?width=650" width="650" height="365"></div><p class="caption">Jeni Haynes on 60 Minutes.<span class="image-source"><em>Source:Supplied</em></span></p></div></div></div><p class="description">A woman with 2500 different personalities has brought her abuser to justice after taking the stand using the voice of six of her “alters”.</p><div class="story-content"><p>Jeni Haynes, 49, developed multiple personality disorder, also known as dissociative identity disorder, after being subjected to sickening sexual abuse by her father Richard Haynes from the age of four.</p><p>&#x201C;My dad inflicted, chose to inflict, severe, sadistic, violent abuse. That was completely unavoidable. Inescapable. And life-threatening. And he chose to do this every day of my entire childhood,&#x201D; Ms Haynes told <i>60 Minutes</i>.</p><p>The abuse was so bad it left her with serious injuries and she&#x2019;s had to endure major surgeries to repair her bowel, coccyx and anus.</p><p>Ms Haynes coped by developing many different personalities.</p><p>Psychiatrist Dr George Blair-West explains that multiple personality disorder can be a sophisticated tactic developed by children who experience significant trauma prior to the age of eight.</p><p>&#x201C;And the thing that seems to push the mind to do this more than anything else is realising you have no way out, that nobody is going to come and get you, nobody&#x2019;s there to save you, you&#x2019;re on your own, and you have to come up with a solution that is entirely of your own,&#x201D; he said.</p><p>&#x201C;There is nothing wrong with Jeni&#x2019;s human mind or any other person who suffers from dissociative identity disorder. Their mind is just coming up with an incredibly sophisticated, clever solution to a scenario that most of us could not begin to understand or relate to.&#x201D;</p><p>Ms Haynes, who grew up in Greenacre in Sydney&#x2019;s western suburbs, has fought for years to bring her father before the courts.</p><div data-ad-size="4x4" data-device-type="web" class="ad-block ad-custom ad-unruly"></div><div class="w650 h365 image media"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://cdn.newsapi.com.au/image/v1/38c9d43d16a448c1291f4ff28b448362?width=650" width="650" height="365" alt="Jeni Haynes (right) and 60 Minutes reporter Liz Hayes."></div><p class="caption">Jeni Haynes (right) and 60 Minutes reporter Liz Hayes.<span class="image-source"><em>Source:Supplied</em></span></p></div><p>She spent 18 years at university, graduating with a degree in psychology, masters in legal studies and criminal justice as well as a PhD focusing on victims of crime.</p><p>Sydney Detective-Sergeant Paul Stamoulis agreed to take on her case nine years ago, but the difficulty was proving it happened.</p><p>Ms Haynes had the physical injuries from the repeated rapes, but it was also decided that she would need to take the stand.
