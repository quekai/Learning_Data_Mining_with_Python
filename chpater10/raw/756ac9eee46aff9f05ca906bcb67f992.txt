  </a></div><div class="c-article-content  js-article-content article__content"><div class="c-article-content__first-element"></div><p>The Hungarian government is offering married couples a 10 million-forint (around &euro;30,590) loan, which they do not have to pay back if they have three children.</p><div class="show-for-small-only "><div class="enw-MPU "><div id="adzone-mpu_1" class="enw-MPU__img advertising js-adzone"></div></div></div><p>The money is available via a scheme that kicked off at the start of July.</p><div id="adzone-outstream" class="advertising advertising--no-label js-adzone"></div><p>What's more, the loan can be spent on anything the couple wants and is interest-free.</p><h2>Is there a catch?</h2><p>Like many other EU member states, the country is suffering from a labour shortage, with workers leaving the country in search of higher salaries.</p><p>Prime Minister Viktor Orban and his right-wing Fidesz party want to encourage population growth via families rather than mass immigration.</p><p>&ldquo;We do not need numbers, but Hungarian children,&rdquo; he said in his State of the Nation address in February.</p><p>"The Hungarian people gave the Government a strong mandate to further expand the family protection system: 1,382,000 people filled out and returned a questionnaire," the Hungarian government's International Communications Office told Euronews.</p><p>"People would like Hungary to remain a Hungarian country, and be family-friendly," it added.</p><p>While the EU average fertility rate is 1.59, Hungary's is lagging at 1.49 children per woman, according to <a href="https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php/Fertility_statistics">Eurostat</a>.</p><p>The loan makes up part of Orban's <a href="http://csalad.hu/cikk/babavaro-tamogatas">Family Protection Action Plan</a>, a seven-point policy announced during the address, which devotes 4.8% of GDP to programmes to support families and encourage childbirth.</p><p>Other points in the plan include a loan programme to support home purchases, subsidies on cars for large families, and a lifetime exemption from personal income tax for women who have raised at least four children.</p><p>Couples must meet specific criteria to get the loan payment in the first place:</p><ul><li>They must be married</li><li>One of the two on their first marriage</li><li>The wife must be aged 18 to 40 </li><li>One of them must have paid social contributions for 180 days in Hungary</li></ul><p>For couples that have one child in a five-year time frame, the interest on their loan is suspended forever and monthly repayments are halted for three years.</p><p>The birth of a second child allows them a further three-year pause on repayments, with any money they have contributed returned and the loan written off upon the birth of a third child.</p><p>If the couple either fails to produce a child in five years or gets divorced, they must repay everything that they have borrowed in four months (120 days).</p><p>They are exempt if they can provide a medical certificate as to why they have not had a child.</p><p>Between its launch on July 1 and July 15, 2,400 families asked for the loan, according to the Hungarian State Treasury, while 14,000 families have so far requested at least one element offered in the plan.</p><p>"The first weeks after the Plan&rsquo;s introduction have proven expectations right," they said.</p><h2>Criticism</h2><p>Its critics say the Family Protection Action Plan is adapted to benefit middle-class Hungarians won't reach the poor, while the country's state secretary in charge of youth and family affairs, Katalin Nov&aacute;k, recently argued that a separate social safety net was available.</p><p>The capacity of current childcare facilities to cope with more children has also been called into question.</p><p>"By 2022 the number of cr&egrave;che places will increase to 70,000 from the current level of around 50,000," the government told Euronews.</p><p>"This means that all parents will be able to find a place for their child in a cr&egrave;che if they wish."</p><p>It remains to be seen if the new plan will remedy Hungary's ageing population &mdash; Orban and Fidesz are certainly banking on it over immigration.</p><h2>Want more news?</h2><div
